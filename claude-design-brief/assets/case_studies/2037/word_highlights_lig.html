Traffic lights disagree on relief : SPD, Greens, FDP: Who wants which measures? Helping citizens, but how? The traffic light coalition is discussing various measures. There is also a dispute over funding. Who demands which relief - an overview. That new relief is coming is certain - what a relief package might look like is unclear. After the traffic light emphasized still unity with the parliamentary group meeting of the government at the weekend in Meseberg, there is now apparently already again discussion need. The ideas of the SPD, the Greens and the FDP diverge, especially when it comes to money. Finance Minister Christian Lindner still sees room for maneuver in the federal budget for this year for the relief package in the single-digit billion range. The Greens, however, do not consider this sufficient: New measures are "certainly not in the single-digit billion range," explained parliamentary group leader Katharina Dröge. Greens and SPD for excess profits tax - FDP rejects SPD parliamentary group leader Rolf Mützenich would not categorically rule out a supplementary budget. "I also offer to think about a supplementary budget then, if it is necessary," he told the radio station Bayern2-radiowelt. The Greens and SPD can also envisage a special tax on excessive corporate profits to finance relief measures - something the FDP rejects as hardly feasible. All three coalition partners and numerous associations and lobby groups had campaigned for their relief proposals in recent weeks. Most recently, they have been moving closer together on a number of points. The Scholz cabinet demonstrates unity. The third discharge package was the focus of the closed meeting. Details are to be decided this weekend in the coalition committee. With which relief proposals are the traffic light parties entering the race? An overview. SPD The leadership of the SPD parliamentary group has presented a list of demands that will be decided on Friday at the group's retreat. These include direct payments for people with low incomes, families, pensioners, students and trainees. No one should have their electricity and gas cut off because they can't pay utilities. Terminations for this reason are also to be prohibited for six months. In order to keep energy prices down, there is to be a price brake for basic electricity and gas. The upcoming increase in the CO2 price is to be suspended for two years, which the Greens have already rejected. There is to be a nationwide local transport ticket for 49 euros. Green The Greens do not have an official list of demands, but the group leadership has proposed assistance for people with low incomes, i.e. basic income recipients, people with small pensions and families with children. These include reforms to citizen and housing benefits. The heating allowance should increase and another energy allowance should be paid out. For families, for example, the Greens want a higher child bonus. Like the SPD, they support a moratorium on electricity and gas cuts and a 49-euro ticket. FDP The Liberals are primarily focusing on relief for the working population. The package should have only a few measures and not a hodgepodge, the group demands. "Policy measures will not take away every imposition and will not be able to offset every burden," a resolution paper says. But it will not work without relieving the burden. At the center is an inflation adjustment in income tax. One-off payments are being considered for pensioners and students - but the FDP generally finds them less sensible. Liberals also support a reform of the housing allowance with more beneficiaries. In addition, gas and electricity prices are to be decoupled so that electricity does not automatically become more expensive when gas prices rise. Major problems are encountered by a coalition government in which there has recently been a lot of friction. ZDF reporter Klaus Brodbeck on the atmosphere at Meseberg Castle. Relief: This is what science demands A scientific study by DIW Econ, a subsidiary of the German Institute for Economic Research, recommends cushioning the high costs without imposing additional burdens on the environment. A heating allowance for housing benefit recipients and a 29-euro ticket for local public transport are suitable. On behalf of the 140-member Climate Alliance, the scientists advise against a gas price cap for basic needs. It does not provide an incentive to save energy. In the case of lump-sum payments such as a heating allowance, on the other hand, the incentive to reduce one's own energy consumption remains. In addition, the study suggests lowering the VAT on plant-based foods and increasing it on meat.
