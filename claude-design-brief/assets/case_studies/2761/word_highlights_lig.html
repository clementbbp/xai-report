The German government wants to implement the agreement on the genocide of the Herero and Nama despite criticism. This is the result of the answer to a small question from the Left Party, which DW has exclusively obtained. The German-Namibian reconciliation has become quiet. A declaration on the genocide in former German Southwest Africa has been on the table since May 2021, but neither government has signed it yet. Descendants of the victims and some politicians called for renegotiations even then. Only talks about the implementation After a long period of silence, the German government has now rejected this. "In the view of the German government, the Joint Declaration is thus fully negotiated, even if talks are still being held on individual modalities of implementation," it says in its answer to a small question by left-wing member of the Bundestag Sevim Dagdelen, which is exclusively available to DW. "It shows arrogance that the massive criticism in parliament in Namibia and the great indignation among the descendants of the victims of German colonial crimes is simply ignored by the traffic light government and shifted to Namibia," Dagdelen tells DW. A number of Herero and Nama representatives see it similarly. They had asked for a meeting with Foreign Minister Annalena Baerbock in December to call for new negotiations. They reject the Joint Declaration because in it Germany accepts only moral, but no legal responsibility for the genocide. Different positions They also criticize the offered reconstruction aid of 1.1 billion euros over 30 years. "This is development cooperation. This is about funding projects and infrastructure, that's okay. But this deal does not deal with the core of the problem, it is not about justice and reconciliation," Nama activist Sima Luipert told Der Spiegel in June. Other Herero, however, share the attitude of the German government: "For reasons of protocol, I do not believe that a foreign government can negotiate an agreement with citizens of another state. Resuming the genocide negotiations would not be accepted by our government and the Herero, Mbanderu and Nama communities," Ueriuka Tjikuua tells DW. He is part of a committee of Herero and Nama representatives that supported and accompanied the negotiations with Germany. Meanwhile, the German government is preparing to implement the agreement. The first funds have already been earmarked in the 2022 budget: 35 million euros for development projects and 4 million for a foundation to promote joint remembrance of the genocide. The funds are part of the 1.1 billion euros in reconstruction aid. Preparations for development projects underway According to the German government, the descendants of the victims are to benefit from the development projects. The German government wants to get involved above all in the areas of land reform, agriculture, water and energy supply, and vocational training. However, it is still unclear when the first projects will be launched: "The projects can only be implemented after the Joint Declaration has been signed by both foreign ministers," a BMZ spokeswoman told DW. The ministry does not want to reveal how concrete the plans for the projects are. The company is in "ongoing contact" with the Namibian government. However, it is unclear whether the still wants to implement the agreement. After negotiations with Germany ended last May, angry Herero and Nama protested in the capital Windhoek. Demonstrators even crowded onto the parliamentary grounds before the crucial debate in September 2021. There were also heated discussions in the plenary chamber: The opposition strongly criticized the treaty, and even some members of parliament from the ruling SWAPO party were dissatisfied. Despite its overwhelming majority in parliament, the government has so far refrained from holding a vote. "The fact that the government has backed down and left it open whether there will be another debate shows the uncertainty on the Namibian side, because people have realized: it's not that simple," German-Namibian political scientist Henning Melber tells DW. Decision in October? The German government's response, on the other hand, says: "The Namibian government is sticking to the draft Joint Declaration even after controversial discussions in the Namibian National Assembly." According to Herero representative Tjikuua, all relevant stakeholders from Namibia want to decide in October whether the parliament should ratify the declaration. In the view of the German government, however, the Bundestag should not vote on the declaration at all: "It is true that the negotiated 'Joint Declaration' is not a treaty under international law and does not require ratification by the Bundestag," the answer to the small question states. Left-wing MP Dagdelen is not at all satisfied with this: "With such a historic declaration, it would be more than appropriate to refer the matter to parliament as well. Even if it's not legally mandatory, at least it would be politically and have high symbolic power," she says. But it is quite possible that all the planning games will remain pure theory for the time being: Namibia is due to hold elections in November 2024. In view of its poor popularity ratings, the government is likely to avoid anything in advance that could cause unrest in the country - and cost it votes. And that could apply to the Joint Declaration as well.
